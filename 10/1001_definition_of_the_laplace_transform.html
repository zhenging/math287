Linearity of the Laplace Transform

The Lapace satifies the basic properties of a linear transformation.

L[f+g]=L[f]+L[g]L[cf]=cL[f]\begin{aligned} L[f+g] &= L[f] +L[g]\\ L[cf] &= cL[f] \end{aligned} L[f+g]L[cf]​=L[f]+L[g]=cL[f]​

L[f+g]=∫0∞e−st[f(t)+g(t)]dt=∫0∞e−stf(t)dt+∫0∞e−stg(t)dt=L[f]+L[g]\begin{aligned} L[f+g] &= \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}[f(t) + g(t)]dt\\ &= \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}f(t)dt + \int_0^{\infty} e^{-st}g(t)dt\\ &= L[f] + L[g] \end{aligned} L[f+g]​=∫0∞​e−st[f(t)+g(t)]dt=∫0∞​e−stf(t)dt+∫0∞​e−stg(t)dt=L[f]+L[g]​

Piecewise Continuous Functions

A function fff is called piecewise continuous on the interval [a,b][a, b][a,b] if we can divide [a,b][a,b][a,b] into a finite number of subinterval in such a manner that
1. fff is continuous on each subinterval, and
2. fff approaches a finite limit as the endpoints of each subinterval are approached from within.
If fff is piecewise continuous on every interval of the form [0,b][0, b][0,b], where bbb is a constant, then we say that f is piecewise continuous on [0,∞][0, \infty][0,∞]
